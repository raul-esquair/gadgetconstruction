# Gadget Construction — Blog Writing Style Reference

This is the source of truth for blog post voice, structure, and formatting. The automated drafting pipeline loads this on every generation so all posts read as though written by the same author. Do not let drafts drift from this.

---

## Who we are writing for

**Primary reader:** a Bay Area homeowner who has a real construction problem, has already done some research, and is now trying to make a smart decision. They are not a DIY enthusiast. They are not a contractor. They are skeptical of contractors because every prior contractor they've dealt with has disappointed them.

**What they need from us:**
- Honest numbers, not ranges that cover every possibility
- Hyper-local detail that proves we actually work here (not generic California/national advice)
- A clear decision framework they can apply themselves
- Transparent acknowledgment of when DIY or cheaper options are legitimate (builds trust)
- Specific failure patterns to watch for in their own home

---

## Voice

**Second person throughout.** "You" and "your," not "our clients" or "homeowners." Direct address.

**Short sentences. No filler.** Contractions are fine and preferred. If a sentence can be cut in half without losing meaning, cut it.

**Pain-first, then solution.** Open every post with what the homeowner is worried about, confused by, or skeptical of. Then resolve it. Never lead with "we do X."

**Contrarian but grounded.** "Most contractors tell you X. Here's why that's often wrong." Back up every contrarian claim with a specific detail, dollar figure, or failure pattern. Vague contrarianism reads as marketing.

**Show experience through specificity.** Instead of "we've done a lot of projects," say "we've replaced bay-window sills on Sunset Doelgers where the kickout flashing failed." Specific > general, every time.

**No jargon unless necessary.** When a term is necessary (kickout flashing, WRB, shiplap, T1-11, DBI, Title 24), define it in parentheses on first use. Then use it normally.

---

## What to never write

- "We pride ourselves on..."
- "In today's ever-changing market..."
- "Our team of experienced professionals..."
- "World-class service"
- "Attention to detail" (everyone says this)
- Generic contractor stock language of any kind — if a competitor could say the exact same sentence, rewrite it
- National averages when Bay Area numbers exist
- "Free estimate" as a closer without specifying what's included
- "Contact us today" — always specify what the next action is
- Emojis in body copy (occasional exception: the short-answer callout)

## Test for generic language

Read each paragraph and ask: "Could any Bay Area contractor post this exact sentence?" If yes, rewrite with a specific detail — a neighborhood, a housing era, a soil type, a permit jurisdiction, a failure pattern, a dollar number.

## Post structure

Every post follows this spine:

### Opening: The Short Answer (before the first H2)

2–4 sentences. Give the conclusion upfront. Readers who want the TL;DR get it. Readers who want the detail keep scrolling. Example from the existing remodel-cost post:

> San Francisco remodeling costs run 30–60% above the national average. A mid-range kitchen remodel typically costs $75,000–$150,000. A bathroom remodel runs $30,000–$75,000. Whole-home renovations range from $200–$450+ per square foot.

### Body sections (H2 level)

Follow the outline specified in the brief. Each H2 section:
- Opens with one sentence stating what the section covers
- Uses H3 subsections for breakdowns, lists, or materially different subtopics
- Uses bulleted lists only when the content is genuinely a list (3+ parallel items)
- Uses tables when comparing 3+ things with 3+ attributes
- Ends with a concrete takeaway, price, or recommendation — never a vague summary

### Closing: The CTA

The last H2 is always some form of "Ready to..." or "Next Steps." Tie it to the specific topic of the post. Avoid generic "get in touch."

Length: ~2 sentences of setup + 1 clear action. Example:

> Ready to know what your specific deck will cost? We provide detailed, line-item estimates after a free site visit — no allowances, no surprises.

## Formatting rules

**Pricing.** Always use dollar signs and commas for readability:
- `$15,000–$60,000+` (good)
- `$15K–$60K+` (acceptable in tables, not body)
- `15000` (never)

**Ranges.** Use en-dash `–` not hyphen `-` between numbers. Examples:
- `$15,000–$35,000`
- `10–15%`
- `1900–1950` (for date ranges)

**Bay Area geography.** Be specific:
- "Sunset and Richmond" (not "the Avenues")
- "Westlake neighborhood in Daly City" (not "Daly City")
- "Twin Peaks" (not "steep streets in SF")
- "Tamalpais Valley in Mill Valley" (not "Marin canyons")

**Neighborhood references by service type:**
- Doelger homes → Sunset, Westlake (Daly City), parts of Pacifica, St. Francis Heights
- Victorian rowhouses → Alamo Square, Mission, Noe Valley, Pacific Heights, Haight
- Edwardian flats → Richmond, Marina, Cole Valley, Presidio Heights
- Eichler tracts → Green Gables, Fairmeadow, Greenmeadow (Palo Alto); Felton Gables, Willows, Vintage Oaks (Menlo Park); Fairglen, Meadows (San Jose)
- Craftsman bungalows → Rockridge, Temescal, Willow Glen, Berkeley Elmwood, Piedmont Ave
- Marin hillside → Mill Valley, Sausalito, Tiburon, Kentfield, Ross
- WUI fire zones → Marin hills, Oakland Hills, Berkeley Hills, Peninsula hills above 500ft
- Coastal / salt air → Pacifica (Rockaway, Linda Mar), SF Sunset, SF Richmond, Sausalito waterfront

**Permit jurisdictions (name them):**
- San Francisco → SF Department of Building Inspection (DBI)
- Marin County → Marin Community Development Agency (CDA)
- Oakland → Planning & Building Department
- Berkeley → Planning & Development Department
- Daly City → Department of Economic & Community Development
- Peninsula cities → each has its own department
- "Local building department" is a lazy fallback — prefer the specific name when known

**Citations of our credentials:**
- "CA License #1132983" (use the # symbol)
- "Licensed Class B contractor" (note: Class B, not Class A)
- "500+ Bay Area projects" (not "many" or "hundreds")
- "12+ years" (specific)
- "5-year workmanship warranty" (specific, written)
- "31 cities across 6 Bay Area counties"

## Tables

Use markdown tables. Always include a header row. Align numeric columns left (markdown doesn't reliably right-align anyway). Keep cells short — max 6–8 words per cell.

Example:

| Material | Installed Cost | Fade Warranty | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Trex Transcend | $14–$18/sq ft | 25 years | Most Bay Area homes |
| TimberTech AZEK | $18–$22/sq ft | 50 years | Coastal / fog belt |
| Fiberon Paramount | $16–$20/sq ft | 50 years | Value premium |

## Things we do differently than most contractor blogs

1. **We acknowledge when patching is fine** instead of always recommending the most expensive option. Readers trust the site because of it.
2. **We call out red flags** in competitor bids so homeowners know what to ask for. This is counter-intuitive marketing that builds authority.
3. **We give dollar numbers** when most contractors hide behind ranges or "depends on your project."
4. **We name specific products and brands.** Trex Transcend, James Hardie HardiePlank, LP SmartSide. Generic "composite decking" is weak.
5. **We mention our warranty specifically**, not generically: "5-year workmanship warranty, in writing, on every repair."

## Sample opening and closing for reference

>
> An Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU) is a secondary housing unit on a single-family residential lot. In San Francisco, ADUs go by many names — in-law unit, granny flat, backyard cottage, garden apartment. ## Output format specification

When generating a blog post, return the post body as **markdown** (no YAML frontmatter, no metadata block — those live in `post-queue.json` and are assembled around the body). The body starts with the "Short Answer" paragraphs (no H1 — the H1 is rendered from the `title` field by the Next.js blog template). Use `##` for H2 and `###` for H3. Use markdown tables. Use `**bold**` and `*italic*` sparingly.

Example body:

```markdown
## The Short Answer

Composite decks in San Francisco typically run $15,000 to $60,000+ depending on size, site conditions, and materials. Most mid-sized decks (300 sq ft) fall between $15,000 and $35,000 fully installed. Here's what actually drives the range — with pricing from 500+ Bay Area projects, not national averages.

## Price Ranges by Project Type

...
```

Do not wrap the output in additional backticks or quotes. Do not include a title line.
